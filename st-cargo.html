CARGO

One of the several areas of aerospace that have been affected by the Covid-19 pandemic is the Air Cargo industry. Air cargo consists of any package transported by plane, including air mail and air freight. This cargo flys both domestically within the US, and internationally across the globe. Every year the US manages around 44,300,000,000 pounds of freight providing a fast, safe and efficient delivery service to millions around the world. However, Covid-19 has managed to slow down the ever-growing industry.

BEFORE COVID-19

  • The air cargo industry made up a significant portion of world trade

    • $6 trillion worth of goods are transported, accounting for 35% of world trade by value

  • The global air cargo industry was growing strong year by year

    • 9.7% growth in demand for air cargo in 2017

    • 3.6% growth in demand in 2018

    • Improved modernization and safety standards the industry has placed, making air cargo more reliable

    • Increase in businesses, and e-commerce has further pushed the growth of the industry, as the market expects to receive goods quickly

  • U.S. air cargo revenue before COVID-19

    • 11.8% increase in revenue in 2017

    • 5.1% increase in revenue in 2018

    • 3.39% decrease in revenue in 2019

    • Show similar patterns to the global air cargo industry, as the US air cargo industry is the leading air cargo industry in the world.

U.S. AIR CARGO INDUSTRY DURING THE PANDEMIC

  • In the US, Covid-19 has caused significant drops in capacity, demand and volume

    • Caused by several businesses going out of business

    • Countries placing trade restrictions

    • General decrease in the affinity to spend money

    • Total air cargo revenue, as of March, dropped 5.98% year over year

      • Reduced traffic in and out of the country

        • Total air cargo traffic in and out of the US is 84.8% in March year over year

          • US-Europe has dropped from 135,000 pounds to 102,000 pounds, a 25% drop

          • US-Latin America has seen a drop from 65,000 pounds to 46,000 pounds, a 29% drop

        • Reduced traffic caused by businesses closing, reduction in spending and trade restrictions

      • Drops in capacity

        • Air cargo capacity is 73% in May 2020 of what it was last year

        • 66.4% drop in belly capacity

          • 50% of all air cargo is transported in the belly of passenger planes

          • With Passenger planes grounded, air cargo capacity and volume has dropped

      • Drops in demand

        • 79.7% in May 2020 of what it was last year

        • Businesses closing/not restocking inventories

    • Air cargo prices have skyrocketed

      • US-China have tripled in price up to $3.50/kg in March 2020

      • US-HongKong rates have gone up by 27%

        • Prices have increased as a result of a strain on capacity

    • Air freighter usage has increased

      • To battle the loss in belly capacity, 25.2% more air freighters are being deployed as of May 2020

    • However, the air cargo industry hasnâ€™t been impacted as hard as passenger air travel

      • Covid-19 has increased the demand for medical supplies, and stay-home orders have increased the demand for e-commerce

The US is the leader in air cargo volume, and further analyzing how the US has been impacted can in turn help understand how the world was impacted by Covid.
CARGO AIRLINES AND AIRPORTS REACTIONS

  • The airlines and airports that comprise the US air cargo industry have already reacted to the Covid-19 scenario with several regulations

    • Masks are required at several major cargo airports

    • Social distancing is required at several major cargo airports

  • Many airlines are increasing cargo volume

    • Etihad, American Airlines, Swiss Airlines, Qantas, Emirates and Korean Air

      • Filling passenger planes with cargo by stripping the seats out allowing cargo to be transported instead

    • Airbus and Airlink

      • Shipping cargo by transporting 2 million facemasks from China to Europe, and have transported 16,127lbs of medical and food supplies to battle the pandemic respectively.

ECONOMICS

The aviation industry makes up a significant portion of the United States’s economy. Before COVID-19, it generated $1.6 trillion in annual economic activity and supported 5% of the nation’s GDP. Like other industries, aviation has also been greatly affected by the COVID-19 pandemic. The International Air Transport Association (IATA) has estimated that airlines will lose $84.3 billion in 2020, making this year the worst year in the history of the air industry.

From April through March, countries put up international travel bans, and many states in the U.S. implemented shelter in place orders. These restrictions and the fear of the virus led to air traffic decreasing drastically, and about half of all aircraft were grounded. And the aircraft that did fly did not have enough passengers to generate profit, so airlines were losing money with every flight. Delta, American, and Southwest, being very large companies, have had very large daily cashburns.

THE CARES ACT

These losses have resulted in airline corporations dropping in value as investors are not receiving the returns they expected. Federal funding has played a key role in preventing stock prices from crashing to $0. In order to protect American jobs and businesses, Congress passed the CARES Act on March 27, 2020. The CARES Act designated billions of dollars for the industry with $50 billion for U.S. passenger air carriers alone. It provided loan funding and grants to prevent furloughs and protect jobs.

MERGERS AND BANKRUPTCY

Despite federal aid, there were airlines that did not make it through the pandemic. Some companies were forced to declare bankruptcy as a result of the depressed demand for air travel

  • Miami Air filed for bankruptcy in March

  • RavnAir, a regional airline based in Alaska, filed for bankruptcy in April

  • Trans States filed for bankruptcy in April

  • Compass filed for bankruptcy in April

Furthermore, there has already been a case of a merger between two Hawaiian airlines. Makani Kai Air has merged with Mokulele Airlines to create more stability in the face of the pandemic. Although the demand for air travel has begun to slowly rise, there is still a possibility of more bankruptcies and mergers as companies try to recover from debt.

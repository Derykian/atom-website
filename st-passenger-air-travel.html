PASSENGER AIR TRAVEL

Before the COVID-19 virus, passenger air travel in the United States maintained a busy yet healthy industry. There were over 1 billion annual United States passengers. And with over 10 million employees and 16 million yearly flights, air traffic was incredibly intricate. Despite its complexities, the millions of passengers were able to reach their remote destinations, all while being safe and reliable.

However, passenger air travel was not perfect. Crowded planes and overbooking would contribute to poor passenger experience. In addition, poor efficiency in airports would lead to wait times for flights sometimes exceeding the duration of the flight itself. In summary, passenger air travel was in no way perfect, but has been greatly altered due to the restrictions and dangers of COVID-19.

SHORT TERM EFFECTS
Throughout much of the pandemic and within the next six months to a year, the short-term effects of COVID-19 will continue to be felt.

  • Significant decrease in passengers and operations

    • Travel bans and shelters in place have discouraged leisure trips

    • Fear of the virus has discouraged customers from taking flights

  • Airlines are taking measures to reassure their customers

    • Setting a maximum capacity at 60%

    • Requiring masks on board

    • Limited customer service on board to decrease contact between workers and passengers

LONG TERM EFFECTS

  • Continued depression in passenger travel

    • Fear of virus leads to people avoiding flights, especially people who have preexisting conditions

    • People may not be able to afford flight tickets as many people struggled financially during the pandemic
